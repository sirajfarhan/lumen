# Concept Visualizer Evidence Model

This reference explains how to turn research into a visual model without reducing a concept to a dictionary entry or a shelf of symbols.

## Research Grounding

Concepts are not one kind of object. The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy treats them through several live accounts. A concept may be a mental representation. It may be an ability. It may also be an abstract content. Classical definitions and prototype accounts expose different failure modes. Theory-based accounts and atomism expose others. For this skill, the practical lesson is that a concept may act like a category. It may also act like a learned capacity. It may act as an inference pattern, a social practice, or an explanatory construct.

Conceptual metaphor theory is useful because abstract ideas often borrow structure from embodied domains. The repair move is to avoid quote hunting. Ask which source domain gives the target concept its spatial logic. Love may borrow from gravity or containment. Anger may borrow from heat or pressure. Debt may borrow from weight, memory, and binding.

Embodied cognition and material engagement justify the house style. Abstract concepts become easier to infer when the visual uses bodily schemas. A push makes pressure readable. A container makes boundary readable. A torn surface makes rupture readable. A repair mark makes return readable. Material Engagement Theory adds that things and artifacts can participate in cognition rather than merely decorate an idea. The visualizer should use tangible materials as inference carriers, but no material should become the default. The concept chooses the carrier.

Affordance theory adds a practical check. A material should suggest what can happen to it. If the viewer cannot infer the possible action, the material is only texture. A strong material choice lets the viewer feel whether the object is being held or changed without adding literal people or labels.

Everyday affordance is the stronger form of that check. The viewer should not only name the thing. They should already know what kind of action the thing permits or resists. Familiar routines lower inference cost because the viewer brings the law of the object with them. A cup fills. A queue waits. A gate opens or blocks. A seal closes a record. A rope pulls only through visible attachment. When a system concept becomes a custom apparatus, the viewer must learn the machine before learning the concept. That violates relevance even when the parts are beautiful and physically plausible.

Whole-situation familiarity is the guardrail inside everyday affordance. The model cannot assemble a believable carrier by borrowing familiar parts from unrelated routines. A viewer may recognize every part while still failing to know the little machine those parts form together. If the whole scene needs explanation, the carrier is not yet ordinary. Search for one known world whose native law already carries the target relation.

Miniature explanatory machines are especially risky for system concepts. They make causality visible, but they often replace a lived public process with a private model of one. Use them only after a known routine or public surface fails. The first search should usually be for an ordinary situation where people already understand how handling changes who gets through and what counts.

Every element inherits that standard. A material earns its place through the action it lets the viewer infer. A color earns its place when it separates roles, binds roles, or carries a world-native status. A connection earns its place when a viewer can understand how force or information travels through it from everyday experience. If the story needs a novel mechanism, first search for a common situation with the same law. Only keep the novel mechanism when the concept would be falsified by every simpler known world.

Physical law turns that affordance into story. Everyday physics gives the image hidden plot. Weight pulls. Tension travels. Friction resists. Heat softens. Water stains. Brittle matter breaks. Shadow proves contact. Use that knowledge as compressed narrative information. Causality lives where one thing touches or changes another. If the image asks for a torn cloth, the viewer should see why it tore. If two things depend on each other, the viewer should see how the support path works. A mark that violates ordinary physics without making the violation explicit becomes private symbolism.

Object recognition adds another gate. Nameable, familiar objects reduce the viewer's first burden. A specialised material experiment may be real, but it often makes the viewer solve the object before meeting the concept. Use ordinary object anchors unless the audience and concept make the specialised object unmistakable. If the image contains a part the viewer cannot name, that part is not compression; it is noise.

Cross-linguistic colexification helps test whether translation is hiding different conceptual neighborhoods. CLICS tracks cases where one lexical form covers multiple meanings across language varieties. Jackson et al. used colexification across 2474 languages to study emotion semantics and found both cultural variation and recurring structure around valence and activation. The visualizer should use language as evidence for adjacency, not as proof that all speakers live the concept the same way.

Cultural psychology and anthropology keep the model from becoming private by default. Many concepts are done between people. They appear through display rules and ritual action. They also appear through obligation and sanction. Repair can carry the concept too. So can silence, exchange, and institutional form. When behavior is part of the concept, the image needs bodies and social distance.

The history of emotions adds time as evidence. Emotional communities and emotional regimes warn against treating present categories as timeless. Emotives make the same warning from another angle. Older stories matter when they show a durable operating shape. Ask what the story lets the concept do. A god may punish or bind. A god may also tempt or regulate. A god may sacrifice, witness, or transform.

Modern theory belongs in the model when it explains function. A feeling may need affective science. A norm may need law or moral philosophy. An obligation may need accounting or anthropology. A capacity may need measurement theory or cognitive science. A market can change the model. So can education, automation, or ranking. Choose theory for explanatory power, not topical coverage.

Conceptual spaces and semantic maps are bridges into visualization because they treat meaning as region and distance. They also make room for dimension, threshold, and neighborhood. Use that spatial intuition carefully. A generated image can borrow from it without pretending to be a formal map.

Relevance Theory gives the compression rule. Sperber and Wilson define relevance through positive cognitive effect and processing effort. For this skill, the visual version is simple: keep the story path that creates the most correct inference with the least visual work.

The same rule applies to the agent's work. Research and comparison are useful while they can change the functional model or the L/A/R choice. They also earn their cost when they change the carrier law or the prompt inspection target. Once those decisions are stable, more branch filling becomes reader and agent burden. Normal durable runs should preserve the selected choice beside the nearest competitor. Add the public or system challenger only when it could have made the default false. Full ladders belong to acceptance tests. They also belong to axis tests and close contests. Zero-caveat work uses the full ladder by default.

Perspective holism is the guardrail that keeps compression from becoming narrow. Frame semantics shows that meaning depends on the scene or frame from which a term becomes true. Construal-level theory shows that distance changes whether people represent an object through immediate concrete features or higher-order relations. Conceptual blending shows that a small visible scene can project structure from more than one input, but only selected structure should project. Together they give this skill one practical rule: preserve the perspectives that change inference, not every possible angle.

The needed perspective spread begins with ordinary use. That standpoint asks what a cold viewer names first. The next standpoint is target law, which asks what must be visible for the intended concept to govern the image. Nearest confusion asks which neighboring concept can steal the carrier. Carrier world asks what counts as lawful evidence inside the chosen R-mode. Consequence scale asks what distance the consequence deserves. It may stop at a trace. It may reach a future. It may enter practice. It may become public or propagate as a system. A model feels holistic when those standpoints agree on one root movement. It feels narrow when one standpoint silently overwrites the rest.

Examples should therefore be used as transfer tests. An example can expose a failure, then become guidance only when the invariant travels. Before turning an example into guidance, restate the invariant across unrelated concept families. Test it through a relation case. Test it again through a trace case. Test it again through a system case. The inference discipline should survive while the carrier and target law change. If the lesson cannot travel beyond the original example, keep it in validation history rather than the runtime model.

Story compression gives that rule depth. A strong novel scene can carry a whole life through one pressure-bearing exchange because the reader can regenerate what came before and what changed after. The visualizer should seek the same effect. The object is not a prop. It is a compressed scene where ordinary matter has enough cause, consequence, and changed future for the viewer to unfold a larger story from a small amount of visual data.

Stakes make the compression alive. A scene matters when the viewer can feel the future that opens if the turn holds and the future that arrives if it fails. The image should make consequence physically plausible. If failure would leave the object-world basically unchanged, the visual story is underpowered.

Target reconstruction is separate from story reconstruction. A cold viewer can recover a rich story and still name the wrong concept. That failure is especially likely near concepts that share a carrier law. The model should name the nearest wrong reading before prompting, then choose the target law that makes the concept govern the image.

Target law is the concept-specific rule that makes the right reconstruction win inside the chosen world. It is not a universal inventory. Derive the roles from the function model and the nearest confusions. Each role should say what must be visible. It should also say what wrong reading it defeats and how it stays lawful inside the carrier. A relation may need an exposed stake and an accountable holder. A public rule may need valid record force. A system concept may need feedback made visible. Target-law recovery adds to story recovery. It also adds to grounded reality. It adds to level meaning and carrier-law coherence. It does not replace them.

Every carrier has its own native story. That story can help, but it can also hijack the target. The model should name what the carrier's ordinary use would make a viewer infer before the target is considered. Then it should either make the target visibly overpower that reading or choose another carrier.

A semantic attractor is stronger than an ordinary native reading. It appears when the carrier's everyday job is already a plausible answer near the target. The test is order of recognition. Before adding the intended concept, ask what the viewer can name from ordinary use and whether that ordinary use would satisfy a neighboring concept. If yes, a counter-cue must be visually dominant at first glance. If the target depends on a small detail while the carrier's usual job fills the frame, the carrier should be replaced rather than explained harder.

Relational concepts need visible asymmetry. A current arrangement is not yet a relation. The image should show why the relation exists. It should show what is exposed by it. It should show who or what can answer for it. It should show what would count as keeping or failure inside the chosen world. Without an origin-accountability pair, a viewer will usually reconstruct a nearby physical or social relation before the target relation.

Visual narrative research adds the event-model rule. Viewers build a mental event from scene information, then update it when new cues imply a change. This skill does not borrow panels or sequence. It borrows the idea that one holistic image can contain enough event information for the viewer to complete the story.

The image should invite bridging inference through ordinary physical knowledge. Leave a gap only when that knowledge can bridge it. The viewer should be able to explain a mark from the visible carrier world.

Mise-en-scene matters because everything visible participates in story. The environment is not background decoration. It is allowed only when it tells the viewer what happened, what is happening, or what could happen next.

Representational carrier is the third visual decision. External representations change what is easy to infer. A tangible object does different work from a trace field. A map does different work from a path. A document does different work from an interface field. They distribute evidence and constraint differently. The skill should therefore choose the carrier that preserves the concept's governing cause with the least distortion.

Use `object_event` as the provisional default because it keeps the house style grounded in material causality. Then test whether the concept is being falsified by that default. A measured concept may need comparison. A remembered or mourned concept may need trace or public inscription. A legal or ritual concept may need code, record, or protocol. A sociotechnical concept may need a system field. The repair is not to add style variety. The repair is to choose the external form that makes the right inference cheap.

R-mode is not the same as L or A. L decides consequence radius. A decides how much evidence the viewer receives. R decides what kind of carrier can bear that consequence at that evidence level. If these axes blur, the model will mistake richer detail for better story or mistake a new surface for a stronger concept.

Surface memory is a carrier failure. A previous working example can teach a proof job, but it must not become the default surface for a later concept. System reach often tempts the model toward familiar operational panels. Those are correct only when the target concept is actually governed by operational state or mediated control. When another proof standard governs the concept, the system should use that world instead.

The carrier should be judged by sign logic. Iconic resemblance can lead when the concept is made readable by material form. Indexical traces lead when marks matter because something caused them. Symbolic or coded forms lead when a rule changes what the carrier does. Comparison leads when distribution or relation is the concept. Movement leads when a path or threshold carries the operation. System state leads when feedback or dependency is the real subject.

The least-upgraded-carrier rule prevents drift. Move away from object-event only when the new carrier makes the viewer more correct. Stay with object-event when a bounded physical event really can carry the governing cause. A new carrier fails when it adds visual novelty while leaving the same inference burden on the viewer.

Carrier-law coherence is the missing layer between R-mode and prompt. Storyworld theory explains that viewers build usable worlds from cues. Affordance theory adds that a world feels enterable when its structures lawfully offer or block action. The practical turn is simple: each carrier has its own proof standard. A document is not judged like an object. A chart is not judged like a trace.

Use the user-facing phrase world coherence when helpful, but keep the runtime question precise: has the selected carrier become a lawful world? Before generation, the model should say what world this is and what makes evidence valid inside it. The viewer should be a legitimate witness inside that world, not an outside decoder.

Carrier laws differ by R-mode. Object-event asks whether matter behaves under constraint. Trace-field asks whether marks could have been made by the event they imply. Measured-surface asks whether comparison is actually possible. Path-space asks whether movement is shaped by access and obstruction. Coded-artifact asks whether marks have record force. Interface-system field asks whether state can visibly change through response.

This is not a new metaphor module. It does not add lore. It protects the carrier's truth regime. The image fails when it borrows a surface without obeying the proof that surface promises.

Visual sequence architecture is separate. It decides whether the story remains one image or becomes several separate images. Event-segmentation research supports splitting only at meaningful state changes. Visual narrative grammar adds the production rule: each image needs a job. Narrative nonfiction adds the labour split: images carry scene-like evidence while text carries compression and linkage.

Use a sequence only when the viewer must update the mental model more than once and one co-present carrier state would become dishonest. Across the sequence, identity should survive inside the same carrier law. The causal thread and viewer task should still feel continuous. Collapse back to one frame when extra images repeat the same inference with different symbols.

For complex sequences, use a continuity dossier instead of relying on repeated prompting. The dossier keeps only what must remain stable for the viewer to believe the run belongs to one world. Use asset-first production when recurring identity or small state differences must survive across images. References may preserve structure or identity while the active concept keeps style authority.

Asset-first is production continuity, not decoration. Provider image workflows and animation production practice converge on the same rule: recurring visual state should be made before the sequence tries to vary it. The asset may be a generated world image. It may be a rendered scaffold. It may be a measured topology or an interface layout. When the tool is text-only, it may be a tightly written shot contract. Its job is to keep the same carrier inside the same topology while the material-light-camera convention preserves one proof standard.

Generated storyboards should therefore default to `asset_first_sequence` whenever same-world identity matters. `multi_image_sequence` remains useful for planning, quick probes, and low-identity-risk series. The distinction is not quality. It is control. A prompt-only sequence can say the same world should persist. An asset-first sequence creates the object that later prompts must inherit.

The asset pack should be small. A world asset fixes the recurring carrier. A topology or shot asset fixes what may not move. State-delta overlays name the beat changes. A material-light-camera lock prevents universe drift. A reference-input plan says how the assets will meet the generator. They may enter as references. They may become edit targets. They may remain inspected scaffolds. If the runtime cannot pass references, record that limit instead of claiming pixel-level preservation.

Asset-first has two gates. One gate asks whether the world stayed itself. The other asks whether the beat changed enough to carry the story. A same-world sequence with invisible deltas is only a continuity success. It is not a storytelling success. Review the beats side by side at normal size and require the cold viewer to point to the changed state without reading captions.

World continuity is stronger than style continuity. Style continuity says the images look related. World continuity says the same carrier law still decides what can happen, what evidence counts, and what change means. A sequence can vary crop, distance, or presentation surface only when the recurring identity and state variables remain inspectable. If an image changes the proof standard, the viewer has entered a new world even when the palette matches.

For sequence work, pair event segmentation with a state-delta lock. Event segmentation chooses the image breaks where the viewer must update the mental model. The state-delta lock says what remains fixed and what changes at each break. The fixed part keeps the world alive. The changed part earns the next image. A beat without a visible delta is redundant. A beat without fixed identity is a different story.

Validate sequences against the asset pack before reverse story. The first check asks whether the generated beats still share the same carrier identity and topology. It also asks whether material and light still preserve one proof standard. The second check asks whether the visible deltas match the state overlays. Only then run reverse story. A cold evaluator should see the image set without captions or notes and reconstruct the root story as ordered change inside one recurring world. The lawful cause of that change should be visible from the images. The check fails when the evaluator reports shared look instead of shared world. It also fails when the evaluator invents separate stories or needs text to explain why the images belong together.

Gestalt perception explains why minimal marks can carry deep structure. A viewer can complete a relation without being shown every part. Use figure-ground and closure to make absence meaningful rather than empty. Use proximity and continuation only when they make the relation easier to infer.

Information design adds a useful warning. Reducing decoration is not enough. Minimalism succeeds only when the remaining visual marks carry the real content. Remove ink that does not change the viewer's inference.

Image prompt research adds a second warning. A good prompt should be structured enough for the image model to preserve intent across runs. Imagegen guidance moves from setting into subject, then into details and constraints. For this skill, that order becomes a movement from concept engine into visual story, then from style contract into inspection target.

Current OpenAI image prompting guidance supports this with a concrete production rule. Use a stable prompt structure. State the intended use. Give the image model the material behavior and the framing constraint that matter most. Iterate with small changes rather than overloading the prompt. For this skill, do not invoke full photorealistic human scenes just to get realism. Prompt for macro material detail and contact mechanics while restating the house-style invariant.

Structural scaffolds are a control layer, not a new style. OpenAI guidance supports stable prompt structure and sketch-to-render workflows when the runtime can use image input. ControlNet and T2I-Adapter show the broader principle: spatial conditions can guide generation when the model path respects them. SDEdit shows the tradeoff. The guide must preserve enough structure to matter, while the final image must still become materially real. Use scaffolds when they clarify the one-frame event. Reject them when they make the image read like a diagram.

Writer owns the prompt surface because the prompt must carry story movement. The concept model supplies truth. Writer turns that truth into the exact control surface the image model receives. Then the concept validator checks the contract shape.

## Source Cards

Preserve a source card for each claim that changes the image. The card should say what the source knows. It should state the supported claim and the condition where that claim applies. It should also name the model change, the visual implication, and the inference boundary.

Weak sources can supply vocabulary or leads. They should not carry the model. A myth summary can point toward a primary story. The story itself or a reputable scholarly source should carry the claim. A popular science interview can explain a theory. The paper, book, or expert profile should carry the evidence weight.

## Evidence Lanes

Language and metaphor should find the implied geometry. If one language treats a concept as something fallen into and another treats it as something carried, the difference is not trivia. A third language may treat the same concept as heat. That gives the model a possible tension.

Culture and behavior should find what people are expected to do. Greeting and avoidance can change the visual model. Public expression and ritual can change it too. Gift and punishment may change the bodies in the image. Confession and mourning may change the distances. Courtship can do the same. Apology and ranking can also matter. Kinship or institutional behavior may change the whole field.

History, story, and myth should find older operating shapes. Ask how the story behaves toward the concept. Does the concept wound or bind? Does it tempt or regulate? Does it expose, redeem, or destroy? The answer matters more than the costume of the story.

Scientific and theoretical accounts should explain operation. Translate theory into mechanism.

```text
feedback / categorization / incentive / allostasis / signaling / memory / equilibrium / threshold / selection / constraint / emergence
```

Modern systems should show the present machinery. Find how the concept is measured or governed now. Also check whether it is monetized or automated. It may be diagnosed, protected, or contested.

## Synthesis Rules

Spatialize disagreement. If one account describes bodily pressure and another describes a social demand, show how body-pressure meets the social field instead of choosing one summary word.

Preserve cultural specificity. A culture should appear through the specific practice or story relation that changes the model.

Choose one visual story. If every source becomes an object, the image becomes a display shelf. Let secondary evidence disappear into that movement.

Ancient stories are evidence of old models, not proof that the old model is the truest one.

Scientific accounts explain mechanisms under conditions. They do not automatically replace language and ritual. They also do not replace myth or moral practice.

## Visual Story Compression

Move from function to form, then from form to minimum. Use visual examples as transfer tests, not as recipes.

A reference image is useful only when it changes a decision. It may open a search lane. It may reveal a viewer-inference failure. It may calibrate the L/A choice. It may give final inspection a sharper comparison target. Keep the prompt governed by the active model rather than a mood board.

Use references through a job extraction. Do not use them through surface extraction. Name the concept or pressure the reference carried. Name the inference it made easier. Name the effort it removed. Name the story scale it earned. Then name the surface that must not transfer. The recognizable object stays out. The composition stays out. The palette stays out. The artist signature stays out. The award context stays out. The media identity stays out. The title and source concept stay out too.

The final creation should be unique. The model may say that a reference proves hidden scale can live in one object, or that a familiar object can carry public meaning, or that a trace can be enough when the force is obvious. The prompt should translate that lesson into a new physical event chosen by the active concept. If the generated image can be described as "like that reference, but for this concept," the run has failed reference independence.

Reference comparison belongs in validation. Compare the generated image against the reference job. Ask whether it creates a comparable inference effect for its own concept with no copied surface. Compare by inference effect. A good result can share the same lesson while looking nothing like the source that taught it.

Reference examples can also calibrate R-mode. One reference may teach that distribution is the carrier. Another may teach that movement and inscription are the carrier. Another may teach that a rule-bound artifact carries a public emotion better than a static prop. Another may teach that a local device is only a portal into an operational field. Extract only the decision principle. Then test whether that principle survives an unrelated concept before it becomes runtime guidance. Do not donate the reference surface to the new image.

```text
force: pull / pressure / gravity / current / voltage
boundary: skin / gate / membrane / law line / threshold / horizon / taboo zone
relation: orbit / braid / mirror / bridge / wound / tether / network / witness circle
temporal cue: strata / growth rings / procession / ruins / seasonal change / palimpsest / forecast layer
```

Every visual choice should be traceable to a source claim or a synthesized function. After that, ask what can be removed while keeping the same story. The image may be poetic when its choices stay earned by the model.

In one-frame mode, the story should fold into one locus. The pressure should enter the same form that changes. The turn should happen at that form. The after-state should remain visible in that form. A row of stations may explain the concept, but it weakens one-frame work because the viewer reads a process strip before they feel the concept. If the story genuinely needs several state changes, move to separate images through visual sequence architecture instead of squeezing a storyboard into one frame.

The compressed form should now be materially believable. Treat humble physical matter as the house vocabulary only when it fits the model. Matter should be chosen for the lawful behavior it contributes. Some matter tears. Some matter transmits or stains. Some resists. Some binds. Some records. Some deforms or fails. This does not mean every image uses the same object. It means every image should feel like one touchable artifact whose matter is under a real kind of force. If the image can only be described as a drawing of an idea, it is not tangible enough.

Zero-caveat compression is stricter. The generated image should contain only marks the inspection note can affirm. If a mark reads first as a stock symbol or diagram device, remove it unless that exact residue is the bounded model. Evidence echoes should be structural, not recognizable trivia.

## Visual Level Framework

Use visual levels to decide how far the story may travel. The levels are a consequence-radius system, not a detail system. A higher level earns its extra world only when it preserves the root event and makes the viewer more correct.

The default is L2 because most concepts need more than a mark. They need a physical event with a future attached. L0 and L1 are valid when the concept truly lives in trace or contact. L3 through L5 are valid when the concept needs practice, public inheritance, or propagation.

```text
L0 trace: changed state only.
L1 contact: force acts on object.
L2 consequence: the event changes what happens next.
L3 practice: repeated use grows around the consequence.
L4 public carrier: people record, inherit, enforce, teach, or mourn it.
L5 system topology: the change propagates through a chain, loop, network, or bottleneck.
```

Each level inherits the root story. The working sentence is:

```text
A force or practice acts on a thing through a constraint, leaving a changed state that alters what can happen next.
```

The level decision starts at L2. Move down only when future consequence would be false or excessive. Move up only when the added layer gives a correct story gain that is worth the added effort. A useful comparison asks what the lower level misses and what the higher level risks. It also asks whether viewers still mention the root object relation after seeing the richer image.

For a normal durable run, this comparison can stay compact. Record the winner and the nearest level that could have won. Add L4 when the evidence suggests public carrying. Add L5 when the evidence suggests propagation. Fill the full L0-to-L5 grid only when the run itself is testing the ladder or when a close contest would make a compact record misleading.

System concepts need an extra check. When the concept lives through propagation, L5 is not decorative richness. It is often the correct story scope. Keep the L2 root event inside the L5 image, but do not let a clean local metaphor hide the relation that makes pressure travel and return.

Choose L2 for a system concept only when the user has explicitly asked for a local metaphor or when the working boundary excludes the loop. In that case, say so in the level decision and name the excluded system ring. Without that boundary, the L2 choice is probably under-scoped.

Feedback systems need closure, not only branching. A fan of routes can show spread, but it does not show return pressure by itself. When the selected concept depends on feedback, the image should show how the changed downstream state reaches back into the next input.

Public and ritual concepts need the same protection at L4. When the concept depends on witness or inherited handling, a private event is not the whole story. The carrier matters because other people preserve or enforce the change. Keep the L2 root event physically readable, but let L4 show how people carry the changed object into a shared order. A private object story can be valid, but the model should say that the public ring is deliberately out of scope.

The cover test protects hierarchy. Cover the added layer. The root story should still read. Cover the root. The added layer should lose its reason to exist. If both layers can survive alone, the image has become assembled rather than nested.

The level source panel is designed by failure. Object-recognition sources protect L0 and L1 from becoming abstract marks. Affordance and causality sources protect contact and consequence. Visual narrative research protects the single-frame event. Peirce and metaphor research protect sign and mapping behavior. Relevance theory, cognitive load, and the coherence principle protect viewer effort. Memorability research protects recall without eccentricity. Design-system thinking protects the same house style across many concepts.

Text is expensive inside the level system. At L4 and L5, text may work when it turns an object into a working record or control surface. It fails when it names the concept from outside the scene. Use the text-off test. Cover the words. The root event should still read.

At A0 through A4, readable prose is an articulation leak. A record surface may contain marks that belong to its world, but those marks should not explain the image. Sentence-like inscriptions turn a coded artifact into a caption unless A5 was selected and the text acts as the hinge of the event.

Prompt control is also text. Internal model labels should stay in the model, not in the generation prompt. Newer image models can render text well enough that a control label may become a visible mark. Translate the decision before prompting. A level becomes its story job. An articulation becomes its evidence job. A carrier becomes its medium behavior. The image prompt should never need visible codes to preserve the model.

The production worksheet is:

```text
Concept:
Functional model:
Root verb:
Root objects:
Constraint:
State change:
Future consequence:
Recommended level:
Allowed added layer:
Layer's story job:
Forbidden decoys:
Text policy:
Cold-viewer pass sentence:
```

The cold-viewer sentence should stay concrete.

```text
X acted on Y through Z, leaving it changed so that W now happens.
```

For L3, add the repeated practice. For L4, add the public carrier. For L5, add downstream propagation.

## Visual Articulation Framework

Use articulation to decide how explicit the visible evidence should become after the story reach is chosen. In user-facing language, call this visual concreteness. In the model, call it representational articulation. The term matters internally because realism is only one form of visual evidence. A trace can carry cause. A schematic relation can carry structure. A tangible object can carry identity. Context can carry use. Text can carry convention when it acts inside the physical story.

The default is A2 because the house style works best when a viewer can name the objects and infer a consequence without being asked to read a full scene. A0 and A1 are valid when abstraction strengthens the relation. A3 through A5 are valid when added visual evidence solves a concrete inference failure.

Explain the two axes as separate questions. Detail language makes them collapse. The L-axis is the distance traveled by consequence. The A-axis is the visibility of evidence at that chosen distance. A small image can be L5 if one visible event shows system propagation. A large-looking image can still be L1 if it only shows contact. A sparse image can be A0 at any reach when the trace carries enough proof. A rich image can be A5 at any reach when the inscription is part of the mechanism.

```text
A0 trace evidence: the viewer receives residue or absence.
A1 relation evidence: the viewer receives simplified object-to-object causality.
A2 object evidence: the viewer receives one minimal nameable object relation.
A3 material evidence: the viewer receives physical behavior under force.
A4 use-world evidence: the viewer receives cropped context around use.
A5 inscription evidence: the viewer receives a physical mark that acts.
```

The A-axis uses semiotic weight. A0 leans on index: a mark points to a cause. A1 through A3 blend icon and index: the image resembles objects while showing causal contact. A5 admits symbol, but symbol is expensive because it depends on convention. Text earns its place only when it acts in the world of the image.

Functional fidelity outranks surface fidelity. The image does not need maximum realism. It needs the right operation. A realistic scene fails when the concept's mechanism becomes less clear. A sparse image succeeds when the viewer can reconstruct the relation without private notes.

Choose the lowest A-level that makes the selected L-level readable. Move upward only to repair a named failure. Object confusion asks for more identity. Force confusion asks for material-causal evidence. Domain confusion asks for a cropped use-world. Conventional operations ask for text only when text acts inside the scene. In a normal durable run, record the selected A-level and the one challenger that best exposes the inference tradeoff. Full A0-to-A5 grids belong to A-axis tests, close contests, and zero-caveat work.

Move downward when richness becomes noise. If viewers praise texture but lose the root relation, the image is over-articulated. If text carries the meaning after the object fails, A5 has become a rescue caption. If context starts a second plot, A4 has escaped the house style.

Non-A5 record surfaces need a prompt-side guard. A ledger can show use through dates, ticks, or worn entries when those marks behave like record evidence. It should reject readable explanatory prose. Otherwise the generator may solve the image by writing the intended interpretation onto the paper.

Story reach and articulation combine freely. L5 does not require A5. A system topology can be schematic when the causal chain is legible. L2 can be A5 when a written mark is the physical hinge of the consequence. The combined decision should read as one sentence: this story travels this far, and it needs this much visible evidence to be understood.

Use a contrast pair before committing.

```text
If I change L but keep A, what farther consequence appears?
If I change A but keep L, what extra evidence appears without changing the consequence?
```

If the answer is the same for both questions, the model has not separated the axes yet.

The articulation worksheet is:

```text
Selected visual concreteness:
Selected articulation code:
Why this A-level was chosen:
Inference failure it solves:
What it adds visually:
What it must not add:
Lowest A-level tested:
Why a lower A-level failed:
Why a higher A-level is unnecessary or risky:
Text policy:
Text-off test:
Abstraction risk:
Realism/detail risk:
Cold-viewer articulation test:
```

For A0 and A1 tests, be especially strict about physical reality. The image may be abstracted while still carrying material action. A pressure mark still needs a material. A schematic relation still needs an object relation. The viewer should be able to say what acted, what changed, or what cannot yet be known because the selected articulation deliberately withholds it.

## Prompt Contract

The final prompt should use one stable contract. This keeps image generation from changing style just because a different agent wrote the prompt.

```text
Use case:
Asset type:
Primary request:
Concept engine:
Visual story:
Style/medium:
Composition/framing:
Evidence echoes:
Constraints:
Avoid:
Inspection target:
```

Each field earns a job. `Concept engine` gives the model. `Visual story` gives pressure, turn, and changed state at one locus. `Style/medium` carries the fixed house style. `Composition/framing` controls simplicity and names the shared locus. `Evidence echoes` allows the one or two extra traces the user wants. `Avoid` should reject process strips and separate stations. `Inspection target` protects the result from drift.

The prompt itself must be drafted with writer and validated with writer. Passing the concept validator is not enough when the prompt is the surface that creates the image.

For a clean writer pass, keep the prompt contract visible as fields. Do not collapse the fields into one paragraph. Treat the labels as control syntax and keep their bodies short enough that the image model receives one pressure-bearing relation rather than an inventory.

Leave a blank line between fields when saving the prompt draft. Each field should feel like one instruction with one job. If a field needs a chain of nouns, rewrite it as a physical relation.

## House Style

The fixed style is `tangible compressed visual story glyph`. Treat it as a contract, not a flavor suggestion.

The image should look like one precise material story on quiet space. It can feel artistic and deep while staying focused. The research should be invisible inside the movement. A few well-chosen material events should show pressure, turn, and changed state around one shared form.

Use `tangible compressed visual story glyph` in the image prompt. Keep the style even when the concept is modern or technical. Keep it when the concept is intimate or ordinary. The concept changes the story relation. It does not change the house style.

Reject style drift. Atlas plates make the concept too busy. Bare logo minimalism makes the concept too thin. Pure abstract line work makes the story hard to relate to. Photorealistic people, rooms, or narrative scenes make the model too literal. Cartoon and anime make the evidence feel too light. Cinematic fantasy turns myth into decoration. Corporate infographic makes the concept too flat. Generic surrealism hides the model under atmosphere.

Use material realism as a comprehension aid. The viewer should be able to read the concept through contact. A thread can tighten. Glass can refract. Clay can keep an imprint. Wax can soften. Salt can dissolve. A shadow can prove that something is physically present. The image should not need faces, costumes, or symbolic props to carry feeling.

Choose the carrier system before choosing the surface texture. Ask which physical or representational behavior gives the concept its best relevance compression. The best carrier makes the source model easier to infer than a generic handmade palette would. It may be ordinary. It may be strange only when the target audience can still recognize it immediately.

Carrier choice follows the same compact logic. Object-event is the default challenger because it keeps the image grounded. Record the selected carrier, object-event's survival or failure, and the nearest non-default challenger. Expand to every R-mode only when the concept could be distorted by a private object event or when the user is testing carrier range.

Then test whether the object is ordinary enough. The viewer should not need the model notes to recognize the visible things. Use the common object that gives the same relation before reaching for a specialist object. If the best object has an unfamiliar part, simplify it until every visible element can be named in everyday language.

Finally test visible causality. Every trace should have a visible maker. A line should come from pressure. A stain should come from contact. An imprint should come from a known object. If the cause cannot be inferred from the image itself, the trace is not carrying story.

Then test physical-law legibility. Follow the force or condition through the scene. Gravity should have a direction. Tension should have anchors. Weight should have support. Heat or water should leave the right kind of material response. Shadow should clarify contact or separation. The image may be poetic, but its event should behave like matter the viewer already understands.

For complex systems, do not add a diagram. Find the event model. A complex concept usually has a physical analogue where a system's future is being decided by visible constraint and consequence.

When the event model keeps failing in generation, build a scaffold before adding more prompt words. The scaffold should expose the spatial relation that keeps being lost. If the available tool can accept an image reference, render the scaffold and use it as a guide. If the tool is text-only, translate the scaffold into the prompt and record that boundary. The scaffold is successful only when direct image inspection shows clearer physical law without diagram drift.

## Readiness Judgment

Iteration evidence is comparative. A candidate does not become stronger merely because it scores well in isolation. The evidence must show both that the requested property moved and that protected properties remained equivalent to the approved baseline. This follows configuration management, change-impact analysis, mixed-initiative interaction, provenance, and human-centered evaluation: state, authority, and comparison must remain inspectable across turns.

Reference evidence is also scoped. Record the job a reference performs, the property it may influence, the property it must not influence, and the observable sign that influence remained inside its boundary. A reference name, path, or similarity score alone cannot prove correct use.

Use the oracle ladder without confusing its layers. Deterministic checks catch objective breakage. Machine proxies reveal likely perceptual risk. Metamorphic and mutation tests show whether protected behavior survives controlled changes and whether the validator catches seeded contract breaks. Independent multimodal readers test reconstruction without the creator's explanation. Structured user review supplies preference and approval. Prospective audience evaluation tests transfer in use. No lower layer certifies a higher one.

A strong concept visualization lets a cold viewer infer something close to the model before reading the explanation. The viewer may not know every source. They should still feel the concept's operating shape and the turn it creates.

A strong run makes beauty serve the model and carries research into the visible surface. Cultures should appear through specific practice. Myths should appear through operating stories. Science should appear through mechanism. The final prompt should feel impossible to write before doing the research, yet simple enough that the viewer meets the story immediately.

A 100 score means no meaningful caveat remains inside the chosen boundary. It does not mean the image explains every possible version of the concept. It means the run narrowed the concept honestly and survived cold inspection without a repair note.
